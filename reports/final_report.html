Oil markets move in regimes. Using daily Brent prices from 1987 to 2022, we detect structural breaks with a Bayesian change point model, associate them with major geopolitical and OPEC events, and quantify the before/after impact for investors, policymakers, and energy companies.

1. Business problem

At Birhan Energies we are asked a practical question: when does the behaviour of Brent prices change, and which events are consistent with those shifts? Investors need risk context; policymakers need energy-security insight; operators need planning signals. A single continuous price series hides regime changes — change point analysis makes them explicit.

2. Data and methods

We analyse 9,011 daily Brent prices (1987-05-20 to 2022-11-14), plus a curated set of 15 events spanning conflict, OPEC policy, sanctions, pandemics, and economic shocks.

Exploratory analysis showed the raw price is non-stationary while daily log returns are stationary and exhibit volatility clustering. For the Bayesian model we aggregate to monthly average prices (427 points) so the discrete switch-point prior is computationally and interpretively tractable.

Bayesian single change point model (PyMC)

tau   ~ DiscreteUniform(0, N-1)
mu_1  ~ Normal(mean, 2·std)     # before τ
mu_2  ~ Normal(mean, 2·std)     # after τ
sigma ~ HalfNormal(2·std)
mu_t  = switch(τ ≥ t, μ₁, μ₂)
price ~ Normal(mu_t, sigma)

We sample with MCMC (pm.sample), check convergence with r_hat ≈ 1.0, and report the posterior of τ and the regime means. As a complementary multi-break detector we also run ruptures (Binseg, L2).

3. Key finding: a dominant regime shift in early 2005

Detected change point: 2005-02-28
95% credible interval: 2004-10-31 → 2005-06-30
Mean before: $21.48/bbl  |  Mean after: $75.78/bbl
Regime shift: +252.8%  |  P(μ₂ > μ₁) = 1.0

The model identifies a sharp, high-confidence break in early 2005. The average monthly Brent price jumps from roughly $21 to $76 per barrel. This is consistent with the mid-2000s demand super-cycle — rapid emerging-market growth and tight spare capacity — that paved the way for the 2008 spike. Nearest curated events to τ:

  • US-led invasion of Iraq (2003-03-20) — -711 days from τ
  • 2008 oil price peak (2008-07-11) — 1229 days from τ
  • September 11 attacks (2001-09-11) — -1266 days from τ
  • Global Financial Crisis (Lehman collapse) (2008-09-15) — 1295 days from τ
  • Arab Spring & Libyan civil war (2011-02-15) — 2178 days from τ

Additional ruptures breaks fall near 1999-11-30, 2005-04-30, 2011-02-28, 2014-11-30, 2021-07-31 — aligning with the late-1990s recovery, Arab Spring (2011), the 2014 OPEC non-cut, and the post-COVID rebound.

6. Limitations and future work

A change point near an event establishes temporal association, not causation. Confounding, anticipation, and simultaneous shocks (e.g. COVID demand collapse plus the 2020 OPEC+ price war) all limit causal claims. A single change-point model recovers the dominant break; real markets have many.

  • Extend to multiple Bayesian change points and/or variance switches.
  • Use a Student-t likelihood for heavy-tailed returns.
  • Add macro covariates (GDP, FX, inventories) in a regression / VAR framework.
  • Compare with Markov-switching models for calm vs. volatile regimes.

7. Conclusion

Brent oil prices are best understood as a sequence of regimes punctuated by identifiable breaks. Our Bayesian analysis finds a dominant shift in early 2005 (+253% mean price), while event-window analysis quantifies large moves around wars, OPEC decisions, and crises. Together with the interactive dashboard, these results give Birhan Energies’ clients a clear, probabilistic lens on how the oil market responds to the world around it.
